To see why we use the uniqueness claim in the product mapping property (twice). Indeed, the function \(f\circ g\colon X\rightarrow X\) is continuous and satisfies
\begin{equation*} \pi_i\circ (g\circ f)=(\pi_i\circ g)\circ f=\pi_i'\circ f=\pi_i \end{equation*}
for all \(i\in I\text{.}\) But the function \(\id_X\colon X\rightarrow X\) also satisfies \(\pi_i=\pi_i\circ\id_X\) for all \(i\in I\text{.}\) By the uniqueness claim of the product mapping property, we conclude \(\id_X=f\circ g\text{,}\) as desired. The proof that \(g\circ f=\id_{X'}\) is nearly identical, now applying the product mapping propery to maps into \(X'\text{.}\)
