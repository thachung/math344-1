  1. Let \(\mathcal{T}=\{\emptyset, X\}\text{.}\) By definition the given \(\mathcal{T}\) contains \(\emptyset\) and \(X\text{,}\) and thus axiom (1) is satisfied. Since any union or intersection involving \(\emptyset\) and \(X\) is equal to either \(\emptyset\) or \(X\text{,}\) it is clear that \(\mathcal{T}\) is closed under arbitrary unions and finite intersections. Thus axioms (2)-(3) are satisfied.
  2. Let \(\mathcal{T}=\mathcal{P}(X)\text{.}\) By definition, \(\mathcal{T}\) is the set of all subsets of \(X\text{.}\) In particular, it contains \(\emptyset\) and \(X\text{,}\) and thus axioms (1) is satisfied. Furthermore, since any union or intersection of subsets of \(X\) is again a subset of \(X\text{,}\) we see that axioms (2)-(3) are satisfied.
